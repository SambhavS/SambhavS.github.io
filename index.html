Pictacular

Pictacular is a mosaic maker. It takes some picture you give it (say, for example, an image of Mario) and recreates it by combining lots and lots of small images. The program has thousands of images to choose from, which helps the mosaic look very similar to the original given image. The naive solution is extremely, extremely slow so I came up with an approach that used pixel sampling and dynamic programming to make it much, much faster.

Pictacular Demo

If you open the mosaic image in a new tab, the full-detail image will render & you'll be able to zoom in and see all the little images that were used in making the mosaic.

Tileman

Tiler is an angsty teen, forever pained by the fact that he lives in a world made solely of tiles. In this Pokemon-style game, Tiler walks around endlessly, soothed only by the material comforts of coins, mushrooms, and shoes. Future updates may reveal that Tiler's world is more interesting than he thinks.

Cardy

Cardy is a tool for sending virtual postcards to your friends via email. You tell it where you are and then it searches Flickr for scenic photos around you. Choose one, add a message, enter an email address and your postcard is sent! This is many times faster than the "naive, hand-written" approach to sending postcards. One interesting thing to note is that email protocol doesn't verify the name of the sender, so it is conceivable that an email may not truly come from the apparent sender.

First Post!

Most first posts are test posts and tests posts are not interesting. I will try to make this post different. I recently read a great book. It is called 'Better Angels of our Nature.' The book talks about all the ways that violence has gone down over the ages. It was around 800 pages which felt very long, especially because I didn't read many books in high school. But each page was interesting and the time was well worth it. Since reading it, I see sometimes see some real life phenomena and remember a theme of his book; this, I believe, is a good indication that his book was important to me and my worldview. The simple fact that early life was absolutely and horrifyingly painful to so many people so much of the time is so dramatic that it can make major pains of life seem trivial when it comes to mind. I also read his second book, 'Enlightenment Now,' but found it less interesting. It seemed like it was mostly full of stuff I agree with and think is true. So full however that it didn't seem like I was learning. But I imagine that if someone disagreed with a lot of its premises (things like humanism, reason, science are sort of core powers of good) then it would likely be a very good book. I hope you consider reading 'Better Angels of Our Nature.' And I hope this test post was less dull than others you may have seen.
